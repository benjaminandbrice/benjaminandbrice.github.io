Tourism

Welcome to Bordeaux! This is a splendid region in the southwest of France with beautiful natural scenery, an exceptional historical and cultural heritage, and world-renowned gastronomy and wine. Explore the immense beaches of the Atlantic coast, the iconic Arcachon Bay with its dramatic tides and delicious oysters, the majestic city of Bordeaux–whose old town called “Port de la Lune” is a UNESCO World Heritage site, the medieval village of Saint-Émilion and its surroundings with gorgeous vineyards and picturesque French villages, and more! If you can, we definitely encourage you to take the opportunity to spend a few extra days (or more)!

Below we list our top recommendations. Bear in mind, this is definitely not an extensive list, and we recommend that you also do your own research! And of course we’ll be very happy to help you with more personal advice and suggestions.

  • Bordeaux
  • Saint-Émilion
  • Wine
  • Bassin d’Arcachon and Atlantic coast
  • Mascaret
  • Dordogne Valley (Périgord Noir)

Bordeaux

Bordeaux is one of the most beautiful French cities with its unmatched 18th century architecture. Wander around the city center (place de la Victoire, place Pey Berland, Grand Théâtre, jardin public…), visit the majestic place de la Bourse and its “miroir d’eau” (pictured), and walk down the quayside along the Garonne river. You’ll be pleased to find that Bordeaux is not an overpriced city, whether it be food, drinks, or activities. If you’re into wine, make sure you visit the brand new “Cité du Vin”, an awesome modern museum dedicated to wine. If you want to read more about what to see and do in Bordeaux, here is a good place to start.

Saint-Émilion

“Saint-Émilion” probably resonates as a Bordeaux vineyard (or wine region) to most French people but it is actually a small town in the vicinity of Bordeaux. It’s absolutely worth visiting, and our wedding venue is only a 5 minute drive from it. Saint-Émilion is a charming medieval village with a rich culture and history, almost like an open-sky museum. The broader “Grand Saint-Émilionnais” area consists of 22 villages and near 1000 wine châteaux, with 8 UNESCO World Heritage sites! In the area you will also find several nice hiking trails from which you can enjoy beautiful landscapes along the Dordogne river. Go check out their official tourism website, it’s really well put together, and you can download several neat brochures there.

Wine

Bordeaux’s reputation as the world capital of wine goes back a couple thousand years! The success of Bordeaux wines is due to its ideal climatic and geological conditions for growing vines, but also a favorable political and economic history. Vineyards and châteaux populating the region help create its unique charm, and the Fall harvest season is the best time to see it when the vines start shifting to orange and red hues. Whether you’re a wine connoisseur or a curious neophyte, Bordeaux has the best wine activities to offer, including:

  • Tours in wine châteaux: many châteaux offer tours of their vineyards and wine making facilities, ending with wine tasting. Absolutely recommended! Our venue itself, the château Saint-Georges, will organize a tour for those of you who are interested: read more about that on the Schedule page. Near Saint-Émilion, the most prestigious wine châteaux to visit include Cheval Blanc and Angélus.
  • Visiting the countryside and charming villages that have a rich wine culture: Saint-Émilion is perfect for that! (see the Saint-Émilion section above). But there are other fantastic wine itineraries (“routes du vin”) that you can read about here (skip to “wine routes”).
  • In Bordeaux city: there are many wine-tasting shops and a couple of wine museums to visit. You’ll also enjoy good wine/food combinations (“accords mets et vins”) in the restaurants and bars of the city. The one place you don’t want to miss is Bordeaux’s prided Cité du Vin, a cool modern museum of wine.
  • There are many wine festivals and special wine tours organized during the year, usually referenced here. But it’s still too early (as of August 2018) to say what there’ll be at the dates of your visit, so check back later!

Bassin d’Arcachon and Atlantic coast


The Bassin d’Arcachon (Arcachon Bay) is a large triangle-shaped bay on the Atlantic ocean, located about an hour drive west of Bordeaux. Brice’s family has a special attachment to the Bassin, going back many generations. One spectacular aspect of the Bassin is its dramatic tides: the bay fills and empties itself completely twice a day. Beyond that, the Bassin offers many cool things to check out, including:

  • Delicious seafood, especially oysters! The photo at the top of this page was taken at La Cabane 57, a place we love in Piraillan, but there’s hundreds of other places to get tasty oysters in the small villages all around the Bassin.
  • Dune du Pilat: Europe’s tallest sand dune is fun to climb, and offers a nice spot to hang out and enjoy the panoramic view, before going down for a swim at the beach! We absolutely recommend going there.
  • Banc d’Arguin: A beautiful large sandbank classified as a natural reserve, which takes 20 min to an hour to get to by boat (there are shuttles from Arcachon and Cap Ferret). Spend a few hours there for a picnic and a relaxing beach session!
  • Cap Ferret: opposite to the city of Arcachon, this small town is at the tip of the peninsula closing the Bassin. We like to go there to get ice cream or waffles, check out the lighthouse, and then head to the beautiful beach of “La Pointe”. Watch out for the strong currents there, and don’t get trapped in one of the beach’s blockhaus, remaining vestiges of World War II.
  • North of Cap Ferret, there are many beautiful beaches off the Atlantic coast that we can recommend going to, such as le Truc Vert (or other beaches near it) and le Grand Crohot. There are also nice beaches and renowned surf spots South of Bassin d’Arcachon, in the Landes, such as Hossegor and Capbreton.

Mascaret


The mascaret (tidal bore) is basically a big wave that travels up the Dordogne river. This tidal phenomenon happens twice a day and is a spectacular thing to see! Locals like to ride the wave on surfboards or kayaks for as long as they can. Click here to read more (in French).

Dordogne Valley (Périgord Noir)

The Dordogne Valley is not quite in the region of Bordeaux, but it’s an exceptional touristic destination, and once in Saint-Émilion you’re already part of the way towards this sublime land sometimes called Périgord Noir. In addition to its splendid landscapes and its villages, among the most beautiful in France, Périgord Noir offers a number of world-renowned prehistoric sites, such as the Eyzies and the Lascaux caves. This summary is really too short, but in order to learn more, you can start here and there! Just a bit further in the Lot, we especially recommend visiting the village of Rocamadour (featured in photo above) and the spectacular Padirac cave.
